STV Minimax: Optimizing Proportional Representation

A smarter way to handle eliminations and avoid vote splitting.

Bottom Line: STV Minimax is a great improvement over STV because it has avoided the vote splitting that happens during eliminations.

How STV Minimax Works

Traditional Single Transferable Vote (STV) uses first-preference votes to determine which candidate gets eliminated in each round. The candidate with the fewest first-choice votes is dropped, and their votes are transferred.

STV Minimax changes this critical elimination step. Instead of only looking at first preferences, it uses all preferences to find out how close every candidate is to being a Condorcet winner (a candidate who would beat everyone else in one-on-one matchups). By looking at the pairwise defeats, STV Minimax calculates how many votes a candidate would need to overcome their worst loss. The candidate furthest from being a Condorcet winner—the one who needs the most votes to win their pairwise matchups—is eliminated first.

The Ice Cream Beach Example

Imagine a straight beach split into 5 evenly spaced zones (Positions 1 through 5). There are 5 voters, one located in each zone. 5 entrepreneurs have set up ice cream stands, perfectly matching the voters' locations. The town needs to grant licenses to 2 ice cream stands.

Naturally, voters prefer the ice cream stand closest to them. If two stands are equidistant, they just flip a coin or pick the one to their left.

Applying STV Minimax

Let's look at the Minimax scores. We compare every stand in 1-on-1 matchups to see what their worst loss is (how many votes they fall short of a majority):

  • Stand C (Center): Beats everyone 1-on-1. It is the Condorcet winner. (Needs 0 votes).
  • Stands B & D: Only lose to Stand C by a narrow margin (2 to 3). (Need 1 vote to tie).
  • Stands A & E (Edges): Lose badly to the middle stands (1 to 4). (Need 3 votes to tie).

Because we use all preferences to measure Condorcet distance, STV Minimax recognizes that the stands on the far edges (A and E) are the furthest from being consensus winners. Stand A and Stand E are eliminated first.

When A and E are eliminated, Voter 1's vote transfers to Stand B, and Voter 5's vote transfers to Stand D. Stands B and D now have 2 votes each—reaching the quota to win!

Comparison: The Flaw of Regular STV

What happens if we run this exact same scenario using Regular STV?

In the first round, Regular STV only looks at first preferences. Because there is exactly one voter at each position, the first-round tally looks like this:

  • Stand A: 1 vote
  • Stand B: 1 vote
  • Stand C: 1 vote
  • Stand D: 1 vote
  • Stand E: 1 vote

It is a 5-way tie for last place.

Regular STV has no mathematical way to differentiate their overall popularity, so it must randomly eliminate candidates. It might blindly eliminate Stand B and Stand D first. If that happens, their votes transfer to the edges and the center, and the town might end up electing Stand A (Position 1) and Stand E (Position 5)—forcing the majority of the beach to walk extreme distances to get ice cream.

By relying only on first-preference votes, Regular STV falls victim to vote splitting. Because the middle stands share the "moderate" voters' lower preferences, they don't get credit for their central appeal in the first round. STV Minimax solves this gracefully by factoring in the holistic Condorcet distance from the very first elimination.

Summary: STV Minimax

Let's review.

Bottom Line: STV Minimax is a great improvement over STV because it avoids the vote splitting that happens during eliminations.

Instead of using only first-preference votes to eliminate candidates, STV Minimax uses all preferences upfront. It puts candidates in elimination order by calculating how far they are from being a Condorcet winner (how many votes they fall short in their worst 1-on-1 matchup).

The Example: Imagine voters and candidates (ice cream stands) spread along a beach. Under regular STV, first-round ties often lead to randomly eliminating moderate candidates in the center. STV Minimax instantly identifies that the stands on the extreme edges are furthest from being consensus winners and cleanly prunes them first, ensuring the winners are perfectly spaced so their voters are always nearby.
